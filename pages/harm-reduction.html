What is Harm Reduction?

Understanding Harm Reduction

Harm reduction is a public health approach that focuses on reducing the negative effects associated with substance use. Rather than promoting abstinence as the only goal, harm reduction acknowledges that people use substances and aims to minimize the potential harm caused by that use.

Harm reduction strategies are evidence-based interventions designed to reduce drug-related harm without necessarily requiring abstinence from drug use. These strategies include health services and community outreach programs.

Core Principles

Meet People Where They Are

We respect individuals' autonomy and meet them at their current stage, without judgment or coercion.

Prioritize Public Health

We focus on reducing infectious disease transmission, overdose death, and other adverse health outcomes.

Respect Human Dignity

Every person deserves respect and compassion, regardless of their substance use status.

Evidence-Based Approach

Our strategies are grounded in scientific research and proven to be effective in communities.

Common Harm Reduction Strategies

  • Naloxone (Narcan) Distribution: Providing access to life-saving opioid overdose reversal medication
  • Fentanyl Test Strips: Helping people identify the presence of fentanyl in substances
  • Syringe Services Programs: Reducing disease transmission through clean needle access
  • Medication-Assisted Treatment (MAT): Supporting recovery with evidence-based medications like methadone and buprenorphine
  • Peer Support: Connecting people with lived experience to provide support and guidance
  • Education & Awareness: Providing accurate information about substance use and overdose prevention
  • Policy Advocacy: Working to change policies that harm people who use drugs

Why Harm Reduction Matters

The opioid crisis has claimed hundreds of thousands of lives. Harm reduction recognizes that:

  • People who use drugs are members of our community deserving of compassion and support
  • Overdose is preventable with access to the right tools and resources
  • Addressing substance use requires multiple approaches, not just abstinence-based treatment
  • Stigma and criminalization increase harm and prevent people from seeking help
